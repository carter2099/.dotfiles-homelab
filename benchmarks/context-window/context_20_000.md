# Interview Transcript: A Conversation with Jordan Chen

**Interviewer:** Alex Rivera
**Guest:** Jordan Chen
**Date:** March 2026

**Alex:** Welcome to the podcast, Jordan! Thanks for being here.

**Jordan:** Thanks, Alex. Happy to be here.

**Alex:** I want to start with the big picture. What's been on your mind lately?

**Jordan:** Honestly, I've been thinking a lot about how technology shapes human behavior. Not in a dystopian way, but the subtle ways our tools change how we think and interact. It's a topic I don't think we examine enough as an industry.


**Alex:** What's a change you've noticed in yourself over the past five years?

**Jordan:** I've gotten much better at sitting with discomfort — in conversations, in difficult emotions, in uncertainty. I used to reach for my phone, change the subject, crack a joke, or find a way to escape uncomfortable feelings. Now I've learned to just stay present. I breathe. I let the silence hang. I let the feeling pass through me instead of trying to avoid it. This has been especially valuable in my relationships. When my partner is upset, my instinct is still to fix it, to offer solutions, to make it better. But what she usually needs is just for me to be there, present and listening, without trying to solve anything. Learning to hold space for someone else's pain without making it about myself has been hard but transformational. The other change is that I care much less about what strangers think of me. In my twenties, I was hyper-aware of how I was being perceived. Now I realize that most people are too busy thinking about themselves to think about me at all. That realization was incredibly freeing.

**Alex:** How do you stay informed about current events without getting overwhelmed?

**Jordan:** I'm very intentional about my media consumption. I subscribe to a few high-quality news sources — I pay for a couple of newspapers and a weekly magazine — and I read them on a schedule rather than in response to notifications. I also use a news aggregator that lets me separate analysis from breaking news. Breaking news is almost always incomplete and designed to provoke an emotional reaction. Analysis, published hours or days later, actually has context and perspective. I try to read the analysis and skip the breaking news. I also have a strict rule: no news after 8 PM. The world will still be there in the morning, and reading about a crisis right before bed is a guaranteed way to sleep poorly. I also make a point of seeking out perspectives I disagree with, but from thoughtful sources rather than outrage-driven ones. Understanding how intelligent people can reach different conclusions is genuinely valuable, and it's a skill that atrophies when you only consume media that confirms your existing views. The goal isn't to be informed about everything — that's impossible and a recipe for anxiety. The goal is to be informed about the things that matter most to me, and to have enough context to evaluate new information critically when it crosses my path.

**Alex:** If you had a year off with no obligations, what would you do?

**Jordan:** I'd spend the first three months in Japan, specifically in Kyoto. I'd rent a small apartment near a temple, take daily Japanese lessons, and spend my afternoons wandering the city. I'd learn to make ceramics at a local studio. I'd eat my way through the city's best ramen shops. Then I'd spend three months learning to sail — maybe in Greece or Croatia, somewhere with beautiful coastlines and reliable wind. I've always wanted to be competent on the water, and a year off would be the perfect time to get serious about it. The next three months I'd dedicate to writing. I've had a novel idea kicking around in my head for years, and I'd love the space to actually try to write it, even if it's terrible. And the last three months I'd spend volunteering — maybe teaching English somewhere, or helping with conservation work. I'd want the year to feel generative, not just consumptive. The truth is that I've never taken a real break. I went straight from school to work, and I've always had something I was building towards. A year of unstructured time is both terrifying and deeply appealing to me.

**Alex:** What do you think is the most underrated quality in a person?

**Jordan:** Reliability. It's not flashy, it doesn't make for good stories, but it's the foundation of every strong relationship I've ever had — personal or professional. If I say I'll do something, I do it. If I say I'll be somewhere, I'm there on time. If I commit to a deadline, I meet it or communicate early if I can't. That consistency builds trust slowly but powerfully, and trust is the currency of all human interaction. I've worked with brilliant people who were unreliable, and the constant uncertainty about whether they'd deliver eroded all the value of their brilliance. I'd take a reliable person over a brilliant one every single time. The other underrated quality is curiosity. Not the performative kind, but genuine, childlike curiosity about how things work and why people think the way they do. Curious people are never boring, they're never stuck in their ways, and they're always learning. They ask better questions and they listen to the answers. I think curiosity is the engine of personal growth, and it's tragically underappreciated in a culture that rewards certainty and expertise.

**Alex:** What's your take on the future of remote work? Do you think we'll go back to the office?

**Jordan:** I don't think we'll see a full return to the office, and frankly I think that's a good thing. The pandemic proved that a huge portion of knowledge work can be done effectively from anywhere, and companies that try to force a full return are going to lose talent to more flexible competitors. That said, I think the fully remote model has its own problems. Spontaneous collaboration is harder, onboarding new team members takes more intentionality, and there's a real loneliness epidemic in remote-first companies that nobody's fully solved yet. The companies doing it best are investing in regular in-person gatherings — not mandatory office days, but meaningful retreats where people actually connect. I think the future is hybrid by default, with the understanding that 'hybrid' looks different for every team. Some teams thrive on two days in the office, others do better with quarterly offsites and async everything else. The key is giving teams autonomy to figure out what works for them, rather than imposing a one-size-fits-all policy from above. The companies that trust their employees to make those decisions will win. The ones that don't will struggle.

**Alex:** What's a habit you've recently adopted that's made a big difference?

**Jordan:** I started doing a weekly review, modeled loosely on Getting Things Done. Every Sunday evening, I spend thirty to forty minutes reviewing my calendar from the past week, looking at my task list, clearing out my inbox, and planning the week ahead. It sounds simple, but it's been transformative for my stress levels. Before I started doing this, I carried a mental load of all the things I was vaguely supposed to be doing, which was exhausting and made it hard to relax. Now I have a trusted system that I know I'll look at regularly, so I can let go of things between reviews. The other habit I've adopted is a digital sunset — I put my phone in a different room about an hour before bed and read a physical book instead. My sleep quality has improved dramatically, and I've been reading more than I have in years. It's remarkable how much your brain appreciates being free from notifications and infinite scroll, even for just an hour. I'd recommend both habits to anyone who feels overwhelmed by the pace of modern life.

**Jordan:** I love board games, especially modern ones that emphasize strategy over luck. My favorite is Wingspan — it's this beautifully designed game about bird conservation where you're building habitats and attracting different species. The production quality is outstanding and the gameplay has real depth. I also love Spirit Island, which is a cooperative game where you play as spirits defending an island from colonizers. Breath of the Wild was transformative for me — the way it encouraged exploration and curiosity over linear progression felt like a revelation. I spent hours just climbing mountains and chasing sunsets, completely ignoring the main quest. That sense of discovery is something I wish more games captured. I also play a lot of Stardew Valley when I want something relaxing. There's something meditative about tending a virtual farm and building relationships with the townspeople. It's my go-to wind-down activity.

**Alex:** Before we dive into your career, let's talk about your background. Where exactly did you grow up?

**Jordan:** I grew up in Portland, Oregon. Specifically, the northeast side, near the Alberta Arts District. It was a great place to grow up — lots of trees, a strong sense of community, and really good food. I think growing up there shaped my values in a lot of ways. It's a city that values individuality and creativity, and that definitely rubbed off on me.

**Alex:** What's the best advice you've ever received about relationships?

**Jordan:** It came from my grandmother, actually. She said, 'In any relationship, the goal isn't to be happy. The goal is to grow together. Happiness is a side effect.' I didn't fully understand it until I was in a long-term partnership. The idea that relationships are primarily about mutual growth reframes everything. When you hit a rough patch, instead of asking 'Is this making me happy right now?' you ask 'Are we growing from this?' It gives you a much longer time horizon and a lot more tolerance for difficulty. The other piece of advice that stuck with me is from a therapist I saw briefly: 'You can be right, or you can be connected.' In disagreements, we often prioritize being right over maintaining the relationship. And being right is almost never worth the cost. It's a simple reframe, but it's saved me from saying a lot of things I would have regretted. I repeat it to myself before difficult conversations. The goal is connection, not victory.

**Alex:** How do you recharge after a really intense period of work?

**Jordan:** I need complete disconnection. That means no email, no Slack, no social media, ideally no screens at all for at least a full day. I spend that day outside, preferably in nature. A long hike, a bike ride, even just sitting in a park with a book. I need to remind my nervous system that the world is bigger than whatever deadline I was working toward. I also sleep as much as I can. After a crunch period, I'm usually running a sleep deficit, and there's no substitute for catching up on rest. The third thing I do is cook something elaborate — a multi-hour project meal that requires focus and patience. It's productive but in a completely different register than work productivity. It reminds me that I can create things with my hands, not just with my brain. I also schedule a proper transition. I don't go from intense work straight back to normal life. I build in a buffer day where I process, decompress, and reflect on what I just accomplished. That reflection is important. If you don't pause to recognize what you've achieved, you just move on to the next thing and eventually you burn out because it feels like you're on a treadmill that never stops.

**Alex:** How has your relationship with failure changed over time?

**Jordan:** Dramatically. In my twenties, I was terrified of failure. I played it safe, I stayed in jobs too long, I avoided stretch assignments because I was afraid of being exposed as incompetent. It was a miserable way to live. The turning point was a project that failed spectacularly — a product I'd spent a year building that nobody wanted. It was humiliating. I felt like a fraud. But something shifted when I realized that the world didn't end. My friends still liked me. I still had my job. The sun came up the next morning. The only lasting consequence was that I had learned an enormous amount about what not to do. After that, failure lost its power over me. I started taking more risks, raising my hand for challenging projects, and speaking up with ideas even when they might be wrong. I failed plenty more times, but each time it hurt less and taught me more. Now I genuinely believe that if you're not failing sometimes, you're not aiming high enough. The key is to fail fast, fail small, and extract every possible lesson from the experience. Failure is data. It's not a verdict.

**Alex:** What's something you believed as a child that turned out to be wrong?

**Jordan:** I believed that adults had everything figured out. I thought that at some point around age thirty, you'd cross a threshold and suddenly understand how life works — what career to pursue, how to manage money, how to be in a relationship, what matters. The reality, I've learned, is that everyone is making it up as they go along. The most put-together adults I know are just better at hiding their uncertainty. There's no moment when you arrive. You're always learning, always figuring things out, always course-correcting. That realization was both disappointing and liberating. Disappointing because it means the uncertainty never fully goes away. Liberating because it means I don't have to have everything figured out either. I also believed that doing well in school was the most important thing, and that the world would reward you proportionally to your grades. That turned out to be hilariously wrong. School teaches you to follow instructions and please authority figures. The real world rewards initiative, resilience, creativity, and the ability to work with other people. None of those were graded in my experience.

**Alex:** What's a film or show that changed how you see the world?

**Jordan:** I'd say 'The Truman Show' had a bigger impact on me than any other film. I watched it as a teenager and it planted this seed of questioning my own reality — not in a paranoid way, but in the sense of asking who's writing the narrative of my life, and whether I'm following a script someone else wrote. It made me more conscious about the choices I was making and whether they were really mine. More recently, 'Everything Everywhere All at Once' hit me hard. The message about kindness being a revolutionary act in a chaotic universe really resonated with me. And honestly, the way it portrays nihilism and then rejects it in favor of radical empathy — I found that deeply moving. I think the best art asks you to sit with uncomfortable questions, and both of those films do that brilliantly. They're entertaining on the surface but they stay with you long after the credits roll. That's the kind of art I aspire to create in my own work, even if I'm writing code instead of making films.

**Alex:** What's something you're trying to unlearn?

**Jordan:** The idea that productivity equals worth. I've internalized this deeply, and I'm still working to undo it. When I have an unproductive day, I feel guilty, anxious, like I've wasted something precious. But that framework is toxic. Human beings aren't machines designed to optimize output. We're meant to have rest days, to be bored, to wander mentally without purpose. Some of my best ideas have come when I was doing nothing in particular. I'm trying to unlearn the Protestant work ethic that says every moment must be productive. I'm trying to give myself permission to be idle without guilt. It's hard. The feeling of 'I should be doing something' is always there, buzzing under the surface. But I'm getting better at sitting with it and not acting on it. I'm also trying to unlearn perfectionism. The belief that if something isn't perfect, it's not worth doing or sharing. That's kept me from starting so many things — writing, creative projects, difficult conversations. I'm learning to embrace the imperfect, the unfinished, the messy. Done is better than perfect. Released is better than hoarded. Shared is better than hidden.

**Alex:** What's a misconception people have about working in tech?

**Jordan:** The biggest one is that it's a purely technical field. So much of what makes a good software engineer has nothing to do with code. Communication, empathy, negotiation, project management, understanding business context — these are often more important than your ability to write clever algorithms. I've seen brilliant programmers fail because they couldn't collaborate effectively, and I've seen mediocre programmers thrive because they understood the problem domain and could communicate with stakeholders. The other misconception is that you have to be obsessed with technology outside of work. I know excellent engineers who go home and never think about code. They have gardens, play instruments, raise children, cook elaborate meals. The stereotype of the 24/7 hacker who lives and breathes technology is not only inaccurate for most people, it's unhealthy. The best engineers I know have rich lives outside of work. That diversity of experience makes them better problem-solvers, not worse ones. Burnout is real in this industry, and the people who survive long-term are the ones who maintain boundaries and identity outside of their job.

**Alex:** What's a skill you think everyone should learn?

**Jordan:** Cooking. Not fancy cooking, just the basics — how to prepare vegetables, cook grains, season food properly, and combine ingredients into a simple meal. It's a fundamentally empowering skill. It saves money, it's healthier than eating out or relying on processed food, and it's a form of creativity that you can practice every single day. There's also something meditative about the process — the chopping, the stirring, the patience of letting something simmer. Cooking connects you to your food in a way that's increasingly rare in modern life. I think the second skill everyone should learn is basic financial literacy — how budgeting works, how compound interest works, how to evaluate a loan or an investment. These are practical life skills that have enormous impact on your quality of life, and they're almost never taught in schools. The third one is how to have a difficult conversation. Most people avoid conflict at all costs, which leads to resentment and unresolved issues. Learning to state your needs clearly, listen actively, and find common ground is a superpower in both personal and professional relationships. I wish I'd learned it earlier.

**Alex:** You mentioned you play piano. How long have you been playing?

**Jordan:** I learned to play piano at age 7. My parents signed me up for lessons after I kept banging on the keys at a friend's house. I hated practicing at first — like most kids — but by the time I was twelve, it had become something I genuinely loved. I still play almost every day, though now it's mostly jazz improvisation rather than classical etudes. It's my way of unwinding after a long day.

**Alex:** How do you think about time management?

**Jordan:** I've moved away from time management toward energy management. The question isn't 'How many hours do I have?' but 'What state is my energy in?' I know I do my best creative work between 8 AM and noon. After lunch, I'm better at meetings, reviews, and collaborative work. Late afternoon is for shallow tasks — email, documentation, organizing. I schedule accordingly. I also protect my mornings ruthlessly. No meetings before noon if I can help it. That's when I do my most important work, and I've learned that if I give that time away, I never get it back. The other mental model I use is the 'eat the frog' approach — do the hardest or most unpleasant task first thing, before your willpower is depleted. I've found that the sense of accomplishment from knocking out something difficult early in the day carries me through everything else. I also batch similar tasks together. Answering emails in one block, not twenty times throughout the day. Making all my phone calls in one block. The context switching cost of going back and forth between different types of work is enormous, and most people don't realize how much it's costing them.

**Alex:** Do you have any rituals or routines when you travel for work?

**Jordan:** I've developed a pretty solid travel routine over the years. First, I always pack a small bag with a sleep kit — earplugs, an eye mask, a travel-sized lavender spray, and my own pillowcase. Familiar olfactory and tactile cues make a huge difference in how well I sleep in a strange bed. I also have a rule that I don't check email for the first hour after I arrive at my destination. Instead, I take a walk around the neighborhood to orient myself and find a good coffee shop. It sets a much better tone than immediately diving into work stuff. For the actual work part of the trip, I block my calendar aggressively. Travel is expensive and disruptive, so I want to make sure I'm getting maximum value from the in-person time. I schedule deep work blocks in the mornings when I'm sharpest, and leave afternoons for meetings and social time. And I always build in at least one evening for myself — a nice dinner alone, a museum visit, or just an early night with a book. Travel is inherently draining, and if I don't intentionally recharge, I'm worthless by day three. The best travelers I know are the ones who are ruthless about protecting their energy.

**Alex:** What's your relationship with social media? Do you think it's mostly good or bad?

**Jordan:** I have a complicated relationship with it. I think social media is an incredible tool for connection when used intentionally. I've maintained friendships with people across the world, discovered communities I wouldn't have found otherwise, and learned about events and opportunities that have genuinely enriched my life. But the default way most platforms are designed — optimized for engagement above all else — is genuinely harmful. The infinite scroll, the algorithmic amplification of outrage, the constant comparison — it's all designed to keep you on the platform, not to make your life better. About two years ago, I did a hard reset. I deleted the apps from my phone, unfollowed everyone who made me feel bad about myself, and curated my feeds to be almost entirely educational or inspiring content. I also set a daily time limit. Now I get the benefits without most of the downsides. The key, I think, is to treat social media as a tool you control rather than a stream you consume passively. If you're not getting clear value from a platform, quit it. I quit Twitter entirely last year and I haven't regretted it for a second.

**Alex:** What do you think is the key to a good conversation?

**Jordan:** Genuine curiosity about the other person. Most people go into conversations waiting for their turn to speak, half-listening while they formulate their response. A good conversation requires a different posture: you have to be truly interested in what the other person is saying, not in what you're going to say next. That means asking follow-up questions, sitting with their answers, and being comfortable with silence while they think. The best conversationalists ask the best questions, not the best stories. The other key is vulnerability. If you share something real about yourself — a fear, a failure, a doubt — it gives the other person permission to do the same. The conversation deepens. It becomes a genuine exchange rather than a polite performance. I've had conversations with strangers that were more meaningful than conversations I've had with people I've known for years, all because someone was brave enough to be real first. I also think good conversations have a rhythm. They ebb and flow between light and heavy, between humor and seriousness. If you're trying too hard to keep things deep, it becomes exhausting. If you never go beyond surface level, it becomes boring.

**Alex:** What's a common piece of advice you disagree with?

**Jordan:** 'Follow your passion.' I think it's well-intentioned but often harmful. The reality is that most people don't have a single burning passion that can be monetized, and the pressure to find one creates a lot of anxiety and self-doubt. What's worked better for me and for people I've advised is to follow your curiosity instead. Passion is something that develops over time as you get good at something and it starts to reward you. Very few people wake up passionate about database optimization or tax law — but plenty of people become passionate about those things after investing years of effort and developing mastery. The other problem with 'follow your passion' is that it implies if you're unhappy or struggling, you just haven't found your passion yet. That's a recipe for constant dissatisfaction. A more useful framework: find something you're reasonably good at that provides enough variety to keep you engaged, pays the bills, and leaves you with enough energy for the things you actually love outside of work. Passion is a byproduct of investment, not a prerequisite for it.

**Alex:** What's a small change you made that had an outsized impact on your life?

**Jordan:** I stopped keeping my phone next to my bed. I bought a simple alarm clock and I charge my phone in the kitchen overnight. That one change improved my sleep quality, my morning routine, and my evening wind-down more than almost anything else I've tried. Without my phone next to the bed, I stopped the late-night scrolling habit. I started reading before sleep instead. I woke up without the urge to check notifications, which meant my mornings became calmer and more intentional. I also stopped using my phone as an alarm, which meant my first and last interaction of the day wasn't with a screen. It soundsminor, but it created a psychological boundary between my digital and physical life that was previously blurred. The other small change was drinking a full glass of water first thing in the morning, before coffee. It wakes up your digestion, rehydrates you after eight hours of sleep, and delays the caffeine hit by long enough that I don't get the afternoon crash I used to get. Both changes cost me nothing and improved my daily experience enormously.

**Alex:** What's a popular opinion that you disagree with?

**Jordan:** That multitasking is a skill worth cultivating. I think it's a myth. What we call multitasking is actually rapid task-switching, and every switch carries a cognitive cost. You're not doing two things at once; you're doing two things poorly in alternation. The most productive people I know are not the ones who can juggle multiple things simultaneously. They're the ones who can focus deeply on one thing at a time for sustained periods. They protect their attention like it's a finite resource, because it is. I also disagree with the idea that being busy is a virtue. Our culture glorifies busyness as a proxy for importance or success. But busyness is often just a lack of prioritization, or an inability to say no. The most effective people I know are often the least busy. They do fewer things, but they do them well. They're intentional about where they direct their energy. I've tried to adopt that mindset myself, and it's made me both more productive and less stressed.

**Alex:** What's your favorite season and why?

**Jordan:** Autumn, without question. I love the transition — the way the light changes, the crispness in the air, the smell of fallen leaves. It's the season of coziness, of sweaters and hot tea and reading by the window while it rains outside. I also love that it's a time of harvest, which connects to my gardening obsession. There's something deeply satisfying about eating a meal made from things you grew over the summer, knowing that you'll have to wait until next year to do it again. Autumn also has the best holidays — Halloween, Thanksgiving — which are low-pressure and focused on community rather than consumerism. And culturally, autumn feels like a new beginning in a way that January never does. Maybe it's the back-to-school programming from childhood, but September always feels more like a fresh start than New Year's Day. I do most of my significant life planning in the fall. There's a clarity to the season that I find very conducive to reflection and intention-setting.

**Alex:** What's the best meal you've ever cooked?

**Jordan:** A few years ago I attempted a full Thanksgiving dinner for twelve people — my first time hosting. I was determined to do it from scratch, including the turkey, gravy, stuffing, mashed potatoes, roasted vegetables, cranberry sauce, and two pies. I spent three days prepping. Things went wrong: the turkey took longer than expected, the gravy was lumpy and I had to start over, and I realized halfway through that I didn't have enough serving dishes. But when we finally sat down and everyone started eating, there was this moment of quiet where the only sounds were forks on plates and murmurs of appreciation. It was deeply satisfying. The turkey was perfectly moist, the stuffing was exactly how my grandmother used to make it, and the pumpkin pie had a buttery, flaky crust. Everyone asked for seconds. It's not the most technically impressive meal I've ever made, but it was the most meaningful. Cooking for people you care about is one of the most fundamental acts of love, and that meal felt like a gift to everyone at the table, including myself.

**Alex:** I always like asking guests — what's a book that's had a lasting impact on you?

**Jordan:** My favorite book is Dune by Frank Herbert. I first read it when I was fifteen and it completely captured my imagination. The world-building is extraordinary, but what really stuck with me is the way it explores ecology, politics, and human potential. I've reread it maybe five times and I find something new every time. The recent movies have been phenomenal too — Denis Villeneuve really understood the material.

**Alex:** What's something you're proud of that isn't related to work?

**Jordan:** I'm really proud of the vegetable garden I've built over the last few years. It started during the pandemic as a lockdown project — I had a small patch of dirt in the backyard that I decided to try planting. That first year was a disaster. The squirrels ate my tomatoes, the carrots were stunted, and I overwatered everything. But I kept at it, and now I have a pretty substantial garden with raised beds, a drip irrigation system, and a small greenhouse. Last summer I harvested over fifty pounds of tomatoes, enough cucumbers to give away to neighbors, and the most incredible sugar snap peas. There's something deeply satisfying about eating food you've grown yourself. It connects you to the seasons and to the actual work of feeding yourself. I've also gotten really into composting and soil health. It's a whole ecosystem of knowledge that's completely separate from my professional life, and I love having that. It reminds me that I'm capable of learning entirely new things, and that competence isn't domain-specific.

**Alex:** What's your favorite way to spend a rainy Sunday?

**Jordan:** That's my ideal day. I wake up naturally, no alarm, and make a big pot of pour-over coffee. I put on some jazz — Bill Evans, maybe, or some Ahmad Jamal — and spend the morning reading in my favorite armchair by the window. No agenda, no pressure. Around lunchtime I make something slow and comforting — a ragu that simmers for hours, or a pot of chili, or a batch of soup that'll last the week. The whole apartment fills with good smells. In the afternoon, I might work on a puzzle or play piano or just take a nap. The key is that I don't try to be productive. Rainy Sundays are for rest, pure and simple. In the evening my partner and I usually cook together — the thing I started earlier becomes dinner — and we watch a movie or a few episodes of a show we're into. I go to bed early, feeling completely restored. The best part is the permission I've given myself to not feel guilty about doing nothing. Rest is productive. It's maintenance. You can't be creative or effective if you're running on empty, and rainy Sundays are the ultimate refueling day.

**Alex:** How has your taste in art or media changed as you've gotten older?

**Jordan:** I've become much more interested in the quiet and the subtle. When I was younger, I wanted movies with big twists, books with dramatic plot turns, music with loud crescendos. Now I gravitate toward works that reward patience. Slow cinema, literary fiction, ambient music. I want art that creates a mood rather than a plot. I want to spend time in a world, not just follow a story. I've also become much more interested in nonfiction. In my twenties, I read almost exclusively fiction. Now I read about as much nonfiction — history, science, biography, philosophy. I want to understand how the world actually works, not just imagine alternatives. But I've also learned that art doesn't have to be challenging to be valuable. There's a place for comfort reads, for predictable movies, for music that's just pleasant. Not everything has to be capital-A Art. Sometimes you just want to feel good. I've stopped being a snob about entertainment. If something brings you joy, it's valuable, regardless of its critical reception.

**Alex:** How do you and your partner handle disagreements about money?

**Jordan:** We have a pretty structured approach that's served us well. We have joint accounts for shared expenses — mortgage, utilities, groceries, travel — and separate accounts for personal spending. No questions asked about what the other person buys with their personal money. That autonomy has prevented a lot of petty arguments. For major financial decisions, we have a rule that both of us have to be enthusiastic, not just willing. If one of us isn't excited about a purchase, we don't make it, regardless of who's paying. We also have monthly 'money dates' where we sit down together and review our budget, investments, and goals. It sounds unromantic, but it's actually become a nice ritual. We pour a glass of wine, pull up the spreadsheets, and talk about our shared future. The transparency means there are no surprises and no resentment. The most important thing we've learned is that money arguments are almost never about money — they're about values, security, and control. Once we understood that, we stopped fighting about dollars and started talking about what we actually wanted.

**Alex:** How do you approach learning a new technology or skill?

**Jordan:** I have a pretty systematic approach now, though it took me years to develop it. But something shifted when I realized that the world didn't end. My friends still liked me. I still had my job. The sun came up the next morning. The only lasting consequence was that I had learned an enormous amount about what not to do. After that, failure lost its power over me. I started taking more risks, raising my hand for challenging projects, and speaking up with ideas even when they might be wrong. I failed plenty more times, but each time it hurt less and taught me more. Now I genuinely believe that if you're not failing sometimes, you're not aiming high enough. The key is to fail fast, fail small, and extract every possible lesson from the experience. Failure is data. It's not a verdict.

**Alex:** What's something you believed as a child that turned out to be wrong?

**Jordan:** I believed that adults had everything figured out. I thought that at some point around age thirty, you'd cross a threshold and suddenly understand how life works — what career to pursue, how to manage money, how to be in a relationship, what matters. The reality, I've learned, is that everyone is making it up as they go along. The most put-together adults I know are just better at hiding their uncertainty. There's no moment when you arrive. You're always learning, always figuring things out, always course-correcting. That realization was both disappointing and liberating. Disappointing because it means the uncertainty never fully goes away. Liberating because it means I don't have to have everything figured out either. I also believed that doing well in school was the most important thing, and that the world would reward you proportionally to your grades. That turned out to be hilariously wrong. School teaches you to follow instructions and please authority figures. The real world rewards initiative, resilience, creativity, and the ability to work with other people. None of those were graded in my experience.

**Alex:** What's a film or show that changed how you see the world?

**Jordan:** I'd say 'The Truman Show' had a bigger impact on me than any other film. I watched it as a teenager and it planted this seed of questioning my own reality — not in a paranoid way, but in the sense of asking who's writing the narrative of my life, and whether I'm following a script someone else wrote. It made me more conscious about the choices I was making and whether they were really mine. More recently, 'Everything Everywhere All at Once' hit me hard. The message about kindness being a revolutionary act in a chaotic universe really resonated with me. And honestly, the way it portrays nihilism and then rejects it in favor of radical empathy — I found that deeply moving. I think the best art asks you to sit with uncomfortable questions, and both of those films do that brilliantly. They're entertaining on the surface but they stay with you long after the credits roll. That's the kind of art I aspire to create in my own work, even if I'm writing code instead of making films.

**Alex:** What's something you're trying to unlearn?

**Jordan:** The idea that productivity equals worth. I've internalized this deeply, and I'm still working to undo it. When I have an unproductive day, I feel guilty, anxious, like I've wasted something precious. But that framework is toxic. Human beings aren't machines designed to optimize output. We're meant to have rest days, to be bored, to wander mentally without purpose. Some of my best ideas have come when I was doing nothing in particular. I'm trying to unlearn the Protestant work ethic that says every moment must be productive. I'm trying to give myself permission to be idle without guilt. It's hard. The feeling of 'I should be doing something' is always there, buzzing under the surface. But I'm getting better at sitting with it and not acting on it. I'm also trying to unlearn perfectionism. The belief that if something isn't perfect, it's not worth doing or sharing. That's kept me from starting so many things — writing, creative projects, difficult conversations. I'm learning to embrace the imperfect, the unfinished, the messy. Done is better than perfect. Released is better than hoarded. Shared is better than hidden.

**Alex:** What's a misconception people have about working in tech?

**Jordan:** The biggest one is that it's a purely technical field. So much of what makes a good software engineer has nothing to do with code. Communication, empathy, negotiation, project management, understanding business context — these are often more important than your ability to write clever algorithms. I've seen brilliant programmers fail because they couldn't collaborate effectively, and I've seen mediocre programmers thrive because they understood the problem domain and could communicate with stakeholders. The other misconception is that you have to be obsessed with technology outside of work. I know excellent engineers who go home and never think about code. They have gardens, play instruments, raise children, cook elaborate meals. The stereotype of the 24/7 hacker who lives and breathes technology is not only inaccurate for most people, it's unhealthy. The best engineers I know have rich lives outside of work. That diversity of experience makes them better problem-solvers, not worse ones. Burnout is real in this industry, and the people who survive long-term are the ones who maintain boundaries and identity outside of their job.

**Alex:** What's a skill you think everyone should learn?

**Jordan:** Cooking. Not fancy cooking, just the basics — how to prepare vegetables, cook grains, season food properly, and combine ingredients into a simple meal. It's a fundamentally empowering skill. It saves money, it's healthier than eating out or relying on processed food, and it's a form of creativity that you can practice every single day. There's also something meditative about the process — the chopping, the stirring, the patience of letting something simmer. Cooking connects you to your food in a way that's increasingly rare in modern life. I think the second skill everyone should learn is basic financial literacy — how budgeting works, how compound interest works, how to evaluate a loan or an investment. These are practical life skills that have enormous impact on your quality of life, and they're almost never taught in schools. The third one is how to have a difficult conversation. Most people avoid conflict at all costs, which leads to resentment and unresolved issues. Learning to state your needs clearly, listen actively, and find common ground is a superpower in both personal and professional relationships. I wish I'd learned it earlier.

**Alex:** How do you think about time management?

**Jordan:** I've moved away from time management toward energy management. The question isn't 'How many hours do I have?' but 'What state is my energy in?' I know I do my best creative work between 8 AM and noon. After lunch, I'm better at meetings, reviews, and collaborative work. Late afternoon is for shallow tasks — email, documentation, organizing. I schedule accordingly. I also protect my mornings ruthlessly. No meetings before noon if I can help it. That's when I do my most important work, and I've learned that if I give that time away, I never get it back. The other mental model I use is the 'eat the frog' approach — do the hardest or most unpleasant task first thing, before your willpower is depleted. I've found that the sense of accomplishment from knocking out something difficult early in the day carries me through everything else. I also batch similar tasks together. Answering emails in one block, not twenty times throughout the day. Making all my phone calls in one block. The context switching cost of going back and forth between different types of work is enormous, and most people don't realize how much it's costing them.

**Alex:** Do you have any rituals or routines when you travel for work?

**Jordan:** I've developed a pretty solid travel routine over the years. First, I always pack a small bag with a sleep kit — earplugs, an eye mask, a travel-sized lavender spray, and my own pillowcase. Familiar olfactory and tactile cues make a huge difference in how well I sleep in a strange bed. I also have a rule that I don't check email for the first hour after I arrive at my destination. Instead, I take a walk around the neighborhood to orient myself and find a good coffee shop. It sets a much better tone than immediately diving into work stuff. For the actual work part of the trip, I block my calendar aggressively. Travel is expensive and disruptive, so I want to make sure I'm getting maximum value from the in-person time. I schedule deep work blocks in the mornings when I'm sharpest, and leave afternoons for meetings and social time. And I always build in at least one evening for myself — a nice dinner alone, a museum visit, or just an early night with a book. Travel is inherently draining, and if I don't intentionally recharge, I'm worthless by day three. The best travelers I know are the ones who are ruthless about protecting their energy.

**Alex:** You've hinted at living abroad. Where did you live, and for how long?

**Jordan:** I lived in Tokyo for 2 years, from 2015 to 2017. I moved there for work — my company had an office in Shibuya — and it was one of the most formative experiences of my life. The culture shock was real at first, but I fell in love with the city. The trains run on perfect time, the convenience stores have incredible food, and there's always something new to discover. I still speak a little Japanese, though I'm far from fluent.

**Alex:** What's your relationship with social media? Do you think it's mostly good or bad?

**Jordan:** I have a complicated relationship with it. I think social media is an incredible tool for connection when used intentionally. I've maintained friendships with people across the world, discovered communities I wouldn't have found otherwise, and learned about events and opportunities that have genuinely enriched my life. But the default way most platforms are designed — optimized for engagement above all else — is genuinely harmful. The infinite scroll, the algorithmic amplification of outrage, the constant comparison — it's all designed to keep you on the platform, not to make your life better. About two years ago, I did a hard reset. I deleted the apps from my phone, unfollowed everyone who made me feel bad about myself, and curated my feeds to be almost entirely educational or inspiring content. I also set a daily time limit. Now I get the benefits without most of the downsides. The key, I think, is to treat social media as a tool you control rather than a stream you consume passively. If you're not getting clear value from a platform, quit it. I quit Twitter entirely last year and I haven't regretted it for a second.

**Alex:** What do you think is the key to a good conversation?

**Jordan:** Genuine curiosity about the other person. Most people go into conversations waiting for their turn to speak, half-listening while they formulate their response. A good conversation requires a different posture: you have to be truly interested in what the other person is saying, not in what you're going to say next. That means asking follow-up questions, sitting with their answers, and being comfortable with silence while they think. The best conversationalists ask the best questions, not the best stories. The other key is vulnerability. If you share something real about yourself — a fear, a failure, a doubt — it gives the other person permission to do the same. The conversation deepens. It becomes a genuine exchange rather than a polite performance. I've had conversations with strangers that were more meaningful than conversations I've had with people I've known for years, all because someone was brave enough to be real first. I also think good conversations have a rhythm. They ebb and flow between light and heavy, between humor and seriousness. If you're trying too hard to keep things deep, it becomes exhausting. If you never go beyond surface level, it becomes boring.

**Alex:** What's a common piece of advice you disagree with?

**Jordan:** 'Follow your passion.' I think it's well-intentioned but often harmful. The reality is that most people don't have a single burning passion that can be monetized, and the pressure to find one creates a lot of anxiety and self-doubt. What's worked better for me and for people I've advised is to follow your curiosity instead. Passion is something that develops over time as you get good at something and it starts to reward you. Very few people wake up passionate about database optimization or tax law — but plenty of people become passionate about those things after investing years of effort and developing mastery. The other problem with 'follow your passion' is that it implies if you're unhappy or struggling, you just haven't found your passion yet. That's a recipe for constant dissatisfaction. A more useful framework: find something you're reasonably good at that provides enough variety to keep you engaged, pays the bills, and leaves you with enough energy for the things you actually love outside of work. Passion is a byproduct of investment, not a prerequisite for it.

**Alex:** What's a small change you made that had an outsized impact on your life?

**Jordan:** I stopped keeping my phone next to my bed. I bought a simple alarm clock and I charge my phone in the kitchen overnight. That one change improved my sleep quality, my morning routine, and my evening wind-down more than almost anything else I've tried. Without my phone next to the bed, I stopped the late-night scrolling habit. I started reading before sleep instead. I woke up without the urge to check notifications, which meant my mornings became calmer and more intentional. I also stopped using my phone as an alarm, which meant my first and last interaction of the day wasn't with a screen. It soundsminor, but it created a psychological boundary between my digital and physical life that was previously blurred. The other small change was drinking a full glass of water first thing in the morning, before coffee. It wakes up your digestion, rehydrates you after eight hours of sleep, and delays the caffeine hit by long enough that I don't get the afternoon crash I used to get. Both changes cost me nothing and improved my daily experience enormously.

**Alex:** What's your go-to order when you're trying a new restaurant?

**Alex:** What's a popular opinion that you disagree with?

**Jordan:** That multitasking is a skill worth cultivating. I think it's a myth. What we call multitasking is actually rapid task-switching, and every switch carries a cognitive cost. You're not doing two things at once; you're doing two things poorly in alternation. The most productive people I know are not the ones who can juggle multiple things simultaneously. They're the ones who can focus deeply on one thing at a time for sustained periods. They protect their attention like it's a finite resource, because it is. I also disagree with the idea that being busy is a virtue. Our culture glorifies busyness as a proxy for importance or success. But busyness is often just a lack of prioritization, or an inability to say no. The most effective people I know are often the least busy. They do fewer things, but they do them well. They're intentional about where they direct their energy. I've tried to adopt that mindset myself, and it's made me both more productive and less stressed.

**Alex:** What's your favorite season and why?

**Jordan:** Autumn, without question. I love the transition — the way the light changes, the crispness in the air, the smell of fallen leaves. It's the season of coziness, of sweaters and hot tea and reading by the window while it rains outside. I also love that it's a time of harvest, which connects to my gardening obsession. There's something deeply satisfying about eating a meal made from things you grew over the summer, knowing that you'll have to wait until next year to do it again. Autumn also has the best holidays — Halloween, Thanksgiving — which are low-pressure and focused on community rather than consumerism. And culturally, autumn feels like a new beginning in a way that January never does. Maybe it's the back-to-school programming from childhood, but September always feels more like a fresh start than New Year's Day. I do most of my significant life planning in the fall. There's a clarity to the season that I find very conducive to reflection and intention-setting.

**Alex:** What's the best meal you've ever cooked?

**Jordan:** A few years ago I attempted a full Thanksgiving dinner for twelve people — my first time hosting. I was determined to do it from scratch, including the turkey, gravy, stuffing, mashed potatoes, roasted vegetables, cranberry sauce, and two pies. I spent three days prepping. Things went wrong: the turkey took longer than expected, the gravy was lumpy and I had to start over, and I realized halfway through that I didn't have enough serving dishes. But when we finally sat down and everyone started eating, there was this moment of quiet where the only sounds were forks on plates and murmurs of appreciation. It was deeply satisfying. The turkey was perfectly moist, the stuffing was exactly how my grandmother used to make it, and the pumpkin pie had a buttery, flaky crust. Everyone asked for seconds. It's not the most technically impressive meal I've ever made, but it was the most meaningful. Cooking for people you care about is one of the most fundamental acts of love, and that meal felt like a gift to everyone at the table, including myself.

**Alex:** What's something you're proud of that isn't related to work?

**Jordan:** I'm really proud of the vegetable garden I've built over the last few years. There's something deeply satisfying about eating food you've grown yourself. It connects you to the seasons and to the actual work of feeding yourself. It reminds me that I'm capable of learning entirely new things, and that competence isn't domain-specific.

**Alex:** What's the most adventurous thing you've ever done?

**Jordan:** I went skydiving once in Queenstown, New Zealand. It's the adventure capital of the world for a reason. I was absolutely terrified — standing at the edge of that plane door, 15,000 feet up, looking at mountains and lakes — but the moment I jumped, the fear turned into pure exhilaration. The freefall lasts about sixty seconds and it's the most present I've ever felt in my life. I'd do it again in a heartbeat.

**Alex:** What's your favorite way to spend a rainy Sunday?

**Jordan:** That's my ideal day. I wake up naturally, no alarm, and make a big pot of pour-over coffee. I put on some jazz — Bill Evans, maybe, or some Ahmad Jamal — and spend the morning reading in my favorite armchair by the window. No agenda, no pressure. Around lunchtime I make something slow and comforting — a ragu that simmers for hours, or a pot of chili, or a batch of soup that'll last the week. The whole apartment fills with good smells. In the afternoon, I might work on a puzzle or play piano or just take a nap. The key is that I don't try to be productive. Rainy Sundays are for rest, pure and simple. In the evening my partner and I usually cook together — the thing I started earlier becomes dinner — and we watch a movie or a few episodes of a show we're into. I go to bed early, feeling completely restored. The best part is the permission I've given myself to not feel guilty about doing nothing. Rest is productive. It's maintenance. You can't be creative or effective if you're running on empty, and rainy Sundays are the ultimate refueling day.

**Alex:** How has your taste in art or media changed as you've gotten older?

**Jordan:** I've become much more interested in the quiet and the subtle. When I was younger, I wanted movies with big twists, books with dramatic plot turns, music with loud crescendos. Now I gravitate toward works that reward patience. Slow cinema, literary fiction, ambient music. I want art that creates a mood rather than a plot. I want to spend time in a world, not just follow a story. I've also become much more interested in nonfiction. In my twenties, I read almost exclusively fiction. Now I read about as much nonfiction — history, science, biography, philosophy. I want to understand how the world actually works, not just imagine alternatives. But I've also learned that art doesn't have to be challenging to be valuable. There's a place for comfort reads, for predictable movies, for music that's just pleasant. Not everything has to be capital-A Art. Sometimes you just want to feel good. I've stopped being a snob about entertainment. If something brings you joy, it's valuable, regardless of its critical reception.

**Alex:** How do you and your partner handle disagreements about money?

**Jordan:** We have a pretty structured approach that's served us well. We have joint accounts for shared expenses — mortgage, utilities, groceries, travel — and separate accounts for personal spending. No questions asked about what the other person buys with their personal money. That autonomy has prevented a lot of petty arguments. For major financial decisions, we have a rule that both of us have to be enthusiastic, not just willing. If one of us isn't excited about a purchase, we don't make it, regardless of who's paying. We also have monthly 'money dates' where we sit down together and review our budget, investments, and goals. It sounds unromantic, but it's actually become a nice ritual. We pour a glass of wine, pull up the spreadsheets, and talk about our shared future. The transparency means there are no surprises and no resentment. The most important thing we've learned is that money arguments are almost never about money — they're about values, security, and control. Once we understood that, we stopped fighting about dollars and started talking about what we actually wanted.
